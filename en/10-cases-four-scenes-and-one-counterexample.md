# Case Studies: Four Scenes and One Counterexample

If a methodology is to stand, it must finally return to the field. Without field evidence, judgment easily becomes slogan; without revisitable cases, structure loses weight.

For that reason, the case section is not an appendix. It is the evidentiary floor of the whole book.

The recurring case of a twenty-person SaaS team redesigning the login and invitation flow is not a journalistic report. It is a pedagogical synthetic case. It stands because behind it stand five sets of public materials: OpenAI's internal use of Codex to build products, OpenAI's productization of harness into App Server, Anthropic's skeletal approach to long-running-agent handoff and recovery, LangChain's treatment of harness as a quantifiable variable, and METR's counterexample showing that boundaries are real.

Together with one explicitly marked pedagogical near-miss in staging, those materials bring the methodology of the first seven parts back down into revisitable scenes.

## 1. OpenAI: The Emergence of Harness as a Problem-Driven Result

OpenAI's public account of using Codex internally to build products is one of the most important starting materials for this book. Its real value lies not simply in “producing a lot of code,” but in forcing a previously abstract question into the engineering worksite: **when a team intentionally minimizes manual patching, what must be written explicitly into the environment in order for the agent to work stably?**

People tend to remember the scale numbers first—five months, around a million lines of code, roughly fifteen hundred PRs, almost no handwritten application code, and real users. But for this book, those numbers are not the center. The center is what made that throughput possible: repo-local docs, default paths, grading systems, observability, locally runnable worktrees, background cleanup, and the continuous rule-writing of experience back into the system.

OpenAI proves first not that the model can finally write a great deal of code, but that if the environment is not written clearly, the agent quickly hits the wall.

That aligns perfectly with Part I: harness engineering is not a priori theory. It is an engineering layer forced out by failure once agents truly enter workflows.

It also supports the seven-layer structure of Part II: tasks become engineering objects at the intent layer; repo-local knowledge becomes the map at the context layer; worktrees, browser access, and logs appear at the tool layer; default paths and rules define the constraint layer; grading and quality loops form the verification layer; background cleanup and rule write-back form improvement.

The shortest way to say it is this: **high throughput is not a reward given by the model. It is compound interest generated by the environment.**

But the case must not be misread as “once models are strong enough, teams naturally enter this state.” It proves the opposite: only after a large amount of environmental rewriting does that state become possible.

## 2. OpenAI App Server: When Harness Rises from Team Experience to the Platform Layer

If the internal Codex case shows that harness is forced out in real software production, App Server shows that once harness matures it does not remain at the level of team habit. It rises into runtime, protocol, and platform capability.

This case matters especially for Parts V, VI, and VII. It shows that harness engineering is not merely a personal skill, but something that multiple interfaces, roles, and integrations must share. That means harness stops being an implicit way of working “known by a few engineers” and begins to be abstracted into stable primitives such as threads, turns, events, approvals, tool execution, and state persistence.

This is also crucial for spillover. Cross-domain generalization is not achieved simply by saying “other industries can use agents too.” What must spill outward is the runtime plus the work surface. If a team cannot clearly define its task objects, state primitives, approval points, and tool surfaces, it is not ready for platformization.

The main point to remember is not “OpenAI made another product,” but that harness has begun to show operating-system-layer characteristics.

## 3. Anthropic: Long-Running Agents Do Not Primarily Fear Inability to Write—They Fear Inability to Hand Off

Anthropic's long-running-agent case is ideal for supporting Parts II and IV because it exposes both structure and control. On the surface it looks like a story about coding. At a deeper level it is a story about why systems lose continuity across session boundaries, misjudge completion, and hand over half-finished work—and how handoff plus verification can make those failures governable.

Its most penetrating feature is not the success flow, but the failure starting point. Anthropic makes explicit that longer context and more continuous calls do not naturally produce reliable long-running agents. Systems show two highly typical failures: over-ambition, in which the agent tries to finish everything in one sweep and dies at the context boundary, and premature closure, in which it looks around briefly and announces that things are basically done.

Anthropic's response is representative: do not keep pushing the problem onto the model. Split the harness. The initializer agent prepares the environment and leaves the initial jobsite. The coding agent then iterates. Progress logs, feature lists, `init.sh`, and git commits form the handoff surface. Browser-based end-to-end verification and clearer feature lists help define what counts as complete.

This is why the case supports a central engineering judgment: **the primary risk for long-running agents is not insufficient capability, but inability to hand off stably.**

## 4. LangChain: Harness as a Quantifiable Variable

LangChain plays a distinctive role in the book. The earlier cases already show that harness matters, but those could still be dismissed as success stories from strong teams. LangChain moves the discussion one step closer to a measurable engineering variable by asking directly: what happens if the model stays the same and only the harness changes?

With the same model, `gpt-5.2-codex`, the score on Terminal Bench 2.0 rose from `52.8` to `66.5`. That does not mean the benchmark *is* organizational reality. But it proves something hard and limited: harness is not only atmosphere or taste. Under the right conditions it can shift the ceiling in quantifiable ways.

More importantly, the case decomposes harness tuning into analyzable mechanisms: traces identify failure modes, middleware forces verification before exit, build-self-verify prevents false closure, loop detection stops the system from spinning in the wrong path, and reasoning-budget plus environmental cues reduce meaningless wandering.

This is why LangChain is central for Part IV. It supports writing harness engineering as a control system rather than as prompt aesthetics.

## 5. METR: The Boundary Value of the Counterexample

Without METR, the case section would lose balance. The earlier four positive cases are strong enough that readers could easily slide into the feeling that the direction is fully settled and only speed remains.

METR interrupts that premature certainty.

Its importance lies in the scenario it studies: experienced open-source developers working in repositories they know well, using early-2025 AI tools to complete real issues. The result was not faster work, but an average slowdown of `19%`. More strikingly, the developers still tended to overestimate how much the AI helped.

This matters enormously for Parts VI and VII. It does not cancel the success cases. It forces us to admit that environment, tacit knowledge, human–machine switching cost, and task shape make agent value highly conditional.

METR's deeper value is that it helps distinguish “AI is useless” from “harness has not yet formed.” When the environment has not been rewritten into an agent-friendly work surface, human familiarity and switching cost can realistically outweigh generative capability. That is not a refutation of the earlier argument. It is its boundary.

The point is not “AI has no value.” The point is this: **not every scene benefits immediately from agents, and boundary itself belongs to methodology.**

## The Missing Kind of Scene: A Staging Near-Miss Where the System Must Stop

The five public cases explain environment, handoff, verification, platformization, and boundaries. But they mostly lack the kind of scene that matters most to ordinary teams: why the system must stop, and who writes the incident back into the environment afterward.

That is why the book adds an explicitly marked pedagogical near-miss in staging in Parts IV and V. It does not replace factual reporting. It compresses previously demonstrated mechanisms back into a chain of actions closer to everyday production:

- How staging signals, logs, and first-line alerts turn “something feels a bit wrong” into a deviation that must be stopped
- How pause authority, rollback authority, and approval authority determine whether the organization is truly controlling risk
- Why retrospectives cannot end with “be more careful next time,” but must ask which layer should have blocked the incident
- How done definitions, verification chains, structural protections, and authorization bands are rewritten after one near-miss

This is not a sixth major case. It is a reinforcement device that adds production feel where the public cases leave a gap.

### Table 2. Success mechanisms, failure mechanisms, and correction mechanisms

| Case | Success mechanism | Failure mechanism exposed | Representative correction mechanism |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| OpenAI internal Codex case | Repo-local docs, worktrees, observability, grading, cleanup | Knowledge not discoverable, default path too weak, bad patterns replicated quickly | Break tacit knowledge into the repository; write rules and quality signals back into the system |
| OpenAI App Server | Persistent threads, unified event flow, tool integration, approval interactions | Harness remains a local trick; state fragments across interfaces | Abstract team habit into stable primitives and runtime interfaces |
| Anthropic long-running agents | Initializer plus coding split, progress logs, feature lists | Session amnesia, premature stopping, unfinished handoff | `init.sh`, structured handoff, end-to-end verification |

| METR study | No stable positive systemic mechanism observed | High switching cost, strong tacit-knowledge advantage, friction in real environments | Prompt patching is insufficient; task division and work surface must be rebuilt |

## 6. Six Harder Judgments

Placed side by side, the five public cases plus the pedagogical near-miss support six durable judgments:

1. Harness engineering was not first invented, but forced out by failure.
2. The seven layers are not an author's arbitrary framework, but a compression of repeated field evidence.
3. Whether a system can keep doing the right thing depends on whether it can stop, verify, and write error back into itself.
4. What is truly scarce is not the individual who uses AI better, but the team that writes environment into organizational assets earlier.
5. Spillover does not happen automatically; it happens only where work surface, verification surface, and responsibility surface are already sufficiently clear.
6. Boundary is not pessimism. It is half of maturity.

## Case-Section Summary

What this section finally completes is not a pile of materials, but the placement of evidence. The key claims of the first seven parts are not private speculation. They are repeatedly forced out by five public scenes plus one explicitly disclosed pedagogical near-miss.

That is why this section is not an appendix. It is the layer that grounds the whole book again.

Compressed to one shortest sentence, the conclusion is this:

**Models can produce answers. Harness determines whether those answers can enter reality.**
