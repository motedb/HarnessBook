## Book Synopsis

### Short Version

Over the past few years, the most common question in discussions of AI and software engineering has been whether models will replace programmers. This book argues that the question lands too early on whether people will be replaced, and misses the deeper shift: **once models begin to participate reliably in real work, the center of software engineering is moving from writing implementations by hand to designing working systems that are executable, verifiable, and governable.**

The book is organized around an emerging but rapidly solidifying core concept: `harness engineering`. If prompt engineering is about expressing intent clearly, then harness engineering is about organizing goals, context, tools, boundaries, verification, memory, and improvement into a system within which an agent can actually work. This is not a book of AI tools, nor a handbook of prompting tricks. It is a methodological work about **how software engineering, team collaboration, and organizational capability are being rewritten in the age of agents**.

### Standard Version

*From Prompt to Harness: An Engineering Methodology for AI Agents* attempts to answer a question that is becoming increasingly unavoidable: when models can already read repositories, call tools, edit files, run verification, and keep advancing tasks, what will software engineering itself be rewritten into?

The book's core judgment is that AI's true impact on software engineering lies not only in model capability, but in how the working environment is reorganized. The gap between teams will not first appear in who connected to models earlier, but in who earlier turned the worksite into a system that agents can reliably enter. For that reason, the value of engineers will not simply disappear. Instead, it will increasingly move upward to another layer: defining goals, organizing knowledge, integrating tools, establishing verification, designing boundaries, arranging handoffs, and writing failures back into the environment.

The book is divided into seven main parts and one case-study section. The first half establishes language and structure: it explains why prompt engineering is no longer enough, why harness engineering is a new language forced into being by engineering reality, and how harness can be decomposed into seven layers. The middle sections enter the realities of software engineering, verification and control systems, and organization and management, discussing how the repository evolves into the operating system for agents, how testing expands into verification for execution systems, and how organizations use pilot matrices, 90-day roadmaps, RACI, ROI, and build-vs-buy decisions to achieve real implementation. The final sections discuss the conditions and boundaries under which this experience spills beyond coding, as well as what it changes and what it does not. The case-study section brings together public materials from OpenAI, Anthropic, LangChain, and METR, while also adding clearly disclosed pedagogical extrapolations, so that the methodology of the book rests on a body of evidence that can be repeatedly revisited.

This book is not chasing a particular tool or buzzword. It is attempting to propose a longer-lasting engineering perspective: **for the first time, software engineering is stably serving two kinds of executors—humans and agents. From this point on, what determines the ceiling of a system will increasingly be not the implementation of a single pass, but whether the working environment can be written into a structure that is executable, verifiable, governable, and handoff-ready.**

### Editorial Selling Points

- This is not an introductory AI book, but a methodological book about the **restructuring of software engineering in the age of agents**.
- It simultaneously covers **engineering structure, verification and control, organizational implementation, failure boundaries, and future judgment**, rather than remaining at the level of tool techniques.
- It includes public cases from OpenAI, Anthropic, LangChain, and METR, while explicitly distinguishing fact, extrapolation, and judgment to preserve methodological rigor.
- It attempts to compress today's most cutting-edge practice into durable judgments that can still be cited over the next few years.
